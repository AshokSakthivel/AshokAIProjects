Domain-Driven Team Topology

A Conway's Law–aligned org structure that ensures each of the 6 engineering squads maps 1:1 to a bounded domain context in the decomposed microservices architecture. Organizational gravity must mirror system gravity — or the monolith will reassemble itself.

🔬

The Conway's Law Imperative

"Organizations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of those organizations." — Melvin Conway, 1968.

At 60PB scale, this isn't theory — it's a war. If ingestion engineers sit in the same team as search engineers, the boundary between those two bounded contexts will be code, not a contract. The result? A monolith that reforms in the cloud. Our topology is designed as an inverse Conway maneuver: we first draw the architecture we want, then reshape the org to match it. Each team owns a distinct domain, publishes a versioned API contract to adjacent teams, and is held accountable for the SLAs of that domain — not the full stack.

Conway's Law Anti-Patterns — Avoided by Design

🚫The Full-Stack Team Problem
If a single team owns ingestion and search, the boundary between them becomes informal. Engineers on one feature start coupling to internals of the other — rebuilding the monolith in disguise.
✓ Fix: Each team has a hard domain boundary enforced by API contracts and separate on-call rotations.
🚫Shared Database Anti-Pattern
A shared RDS/DynamoDB table accessed by multiple teams destroys bounded context. Teams can't evolve their schema independently, creating coordinated deployment dependencies at 60PB scale.
✓ Fix: Each domain team owns its own datastore. Cross-domain reads happen via published APIs only.
🚫Platform Team as Bottleneck
Platform teams that gate every infrastructure change become the monolith's new form — a human bottleneck. When 50 engineers depend on 10 to deploy, velocity collapses under modernization load.
✓ Fix: T6 publishes self-service golden paths. Teams use IaC modules and deploy independently without T6 approvals.
🚫E-Discovery as an After-Thought
Compliance features bolted onto a search team create regulatory risk. When a legal hold must be applied in <4 hours across 60PB, it cannot compete for backlog priority with index performance tuning.
✓ Fix: T4 is a dedicated compliance-first team with its own roadmap, SLAs, and direct legal stakeholder relationships.
🚫FinOps as a Finance Function
Cloud cost managed by finance rather than engineering leads to retroactive optimization. At $24,800/day, every lifecycle policy misconfiguration is immediately material — this must be an engineering domain.
✓ Fix: T3 is an engineering team with code ownership of lifecycle policies, not a reporting function.
🚫The Heroic Individual Dependency
When architecture knowledge lives in one person's head, team topology doesn't matter. The monolith's decomposition stalls when a Staff Engineer leaves or goes on-call for the entire system.
✓ Fix: Each team has 2+ engineers with deep domain ownership. Architecture decision records (ADRs) are mandatory.
